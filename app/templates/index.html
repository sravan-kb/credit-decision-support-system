Understanding Risk Categories

P1

Very Low Risk

Strong credit history, healthy income, and minimal delinquency signals. Highly likely to be approved with favorable terms.

P2

Low Risk

Generally reliable credit behavior with minor irregularities. Approval is likely under standard terms.

P3

Medium Risk

Some signs of financial stress or inconsistent repayment history. Requires closer review before approval.

P4

High Risk

Significant delinquency history or unstable financial indicators. High likelihood of rejection or added scrutiny.

How Banks Use This in Practice

P1 and P2 are the "safe zone" — a bank can approve these customers by default without much scrutiny. P4 customers, if approved, directly increase a bank's NPA (Non-Performing Assets) — loans that stop being repaid — which is a metric banks are closely regulated on.

The real strategic lever is "Risk Appetite": a bank that has already met its lending targets for the period will often approve only P1 customers, staying conservative. A bank under pressure to grow its loan book faster will widen approval to include P2 and even P3 customers, deliberately accepting more risk to hit growth targets. P1/P2 "cover the safe risk" — P3/P4 are the categories a bank dips into when it needs to hit a number.

Note: this P1–P4 scheme reflects a common internal bank customer-risk-tiering approach used in real credit risk modeling — it is not an RBI-mandated grading scale (unlike the CIBIL score itself, which is standardized 300–900 nationwide). Naming and exact bucket boundaries vary bank to bank.
